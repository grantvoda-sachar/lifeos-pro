NOVA

Where the music stopped — 07 October 2023

A dawn gathering of thousands who came to dance for peace became the deadliest single site of the October 7 attacks. This private tour retraces that day with care — a journey of remembrance for those who came to bear witness.

What Nova Was

A festival of peace, love and dance

Nova was built on the psytrance ideals of unity and connection. Young people travelled from across Israel and the world to greet the sunrise together over the Sukkot holiday weekend — a field of music, art installations, and open-air dance floors under the desert sky.

By sunrise on 7 October, the border only a few kilometres to the west had been breached in scores of places. What began as sirens and distant rockets became, within an hour, the deadliest terror attack in Israel's history. The people here were unarmed civilians — dancers, medics, bartenders, photographers, friends.

This page tells their story with care: the sequence of that morning, the geography that shaped it, the acts of courage within it, and how the site is honoured — and visited — today.

At a glance

Facts every visitor should know

The site is now the Nova Festival Victims Memorial at the Re'im parking lot — free and open to the public.

Red anemones (kalaniot), which bloom in this forest each winter, are the memorial's symbol for the young lives lost.

Through the hostage releases that concluded in late 2025, the living survivors from Nova returned home, and nearly all of the fallen were brought back to be laid to rest.

The Morning of 7 October 2023

A reconstructed timeline

Times are local and approximate, drawn largely from the official Israeli investigation as reported by The Times of Israel, Ynet, CNN and others. They reflect successive waves rather than a single assault.

6:29–6:30 am · The rockets begin
A massive rocket barrage from Gaza streaks over the site. The music is cut as rockets become visible overhead — the opening of a wider assault of thousands of rockets and a mass border breach.
~6:35 am · Evacuation ordered
The senior police officer on duty orders the festival shut down and evacuated because of the unusually heavy rocket fire. Minutes later, officers are pulled away to defend nearby towns also under attack.
6:30–7:00 am · The border is breached
Hamas-led militants cross into Israel by vehicle, motorcycle and motorised paraglider. An IDF report later estimates roughly 6,000 attackers breached the border that morning across some 119 points.
Elsewhere · by sea
~6:29–7:30 am · The seaborne assault at Zikim
On the coast north of Gaza, naval commandos approach Zikim beach in small boats. The Israeli navy destroys most of the flotilla at sea; the few who reach shore are stopped at the Zikim base — one of the few places the attack is largely repelled at its entry point.
Elsewhere · Sderot
~6:58–7:00 am · The Sderot police station is seized
Around 40 gunmen enter the town of Sderot and take over the police station, turning it into a fortress. A day-long battle by police, IDF, Yamam and Shin Bet ends only when the building is demolished overnight.
~7:00 am · Mass flight onto Route 232
A second siren sends festival-goers running for their cars. Thousands stream onto Route 232 and the surrounding dirt roads — the only designated way out.
~8:00 am · The roads become the trap
By now about 90% of attendees have left the grounds, but many are caught in traffic. A militant force — roughly 110 fighters in some 14 pickups and on motorcycles — advances toward the area, having captured junctions along the highway.
8:12–8:20 am · Roadblock overrun
Militants open fire on an under-manned police roadblock on Route 232 and fire an RPG, then storm past it onto the festival grounds and the escape roads. Officers open a dirt track east into the fields, a decision that lets thousands escape on foot.
Morning · The roadside shelters
Fleeing attendees shelter in small concrete roadside bomb shelters along Route 232. Several become sites of massacre — later known as the "shelters of death." It is here that Aner Shapira and Hersh Goldberg-Polin make their stand (below).
~10:00 am · The scale becomes clear
The IDF General Staff does not grasp that a mass-casualty event is unfolding at Nova until around this hour — roughly three hours in — a central failure identified by the later investigation.
~11:20 am · First troops arrive
After a soldier on leave phones his commander, a Givati Brigade battalion commander rushes in with about 11 soldiers and begins clearing the area.
~1:30 pm · The grounds are secured
It takes until roughly early afternoon for the IDF to regain full control of the festival site. Clearing operations across the Gaza-envelope region continue for days. 44 people have been taken hostage into Gaza.

The Region

Geography of the morning

The festival sat in the Gaza Envelope (Otef Aza), about 500 m east of Route 232 and ~5 km from the border. Escape routes ran north and south along a single road — and, for many, east across open fields to safety.

Key Locations

Places that hold the story

The Grounds

The festival field

An open field and eucalyptus grove by the Re'im parking lot. Dance floors, tents and art installations stood where the memorial's photographs and anemone sculptures are now planted.

The Only Road

Route 232

The single north–south highway hugging the Gaza border. Directed onto it around 7 am, fleeing attendees met militants who had already seized its junctions near Sa'ad, Alumim, Be'eri and Re'im.

The Shelters

The "shelters of death"

Small concrete roadside rocket shelters (~4×2 m) built against Gaza rocket fire. On 7 October, groups crowded inside as grenades were thrown in. CNN identified at least four along this stretch.

Toward Safety

The eastern fields

The route that saved the most lives: a dirt track opened east into agricultural fields, away from Gaza — thousands crossed on foot toward Moshav Patish and on to Beersheba.

Neighbours

The kibbutzim

Re'im, Alumim, Be'eri, Sa'ad and Kissufim ring the site. Several were attacked the same morning; some communities' civilian defence teams held off the assault.

Refuge

Moshav Patish

The farming community to the east that took in and treated many survivors, reconnecting them with their families in the hours after they fled.

Acts of Courage

Light within the darkness

Amid the horror, ordinary people did extraordinary things — soldiers on leave, volunteers, and strangers who turned back toward danger to save others.

Aner Shapira, 22

An off-duty soldier who took position at the mouth of a roadside shelter near Re'im where roughly two dozen young people had hidden. As militants threw grenades inside, he caught and hurled them back out — about seven times — before the eighth took his life. He is credited with saving many of those beside him.

Hersh Goldberg-Polin, 23

American-Israeli, in that same shelter, he helped throw grenades back out and lost part of his left arm before being taken hostage. Held for nearly eleven months, his parents became global voices for the hostages. He was killed in captivity; his body was recovered in August 2024. His story became one of the most recognised of the entire crisis.

The ZAKA volunteers

Israel's volunteer emergency and body-recovery organisation mounted its largest-ever mobilisation, tending to and recovering the dead from the grounds and the roads — civilians, not soldiers. Many now live with the weight of what they carried; a large share have struggled with PTSD.

The rescuers who turned back

Off-duty police and soldiers, and civilians who had already escaped, drove back into the danger zone again and again to ferry the trapped to safety — some at the cost of their own freedom or lives. Farmer Rami Davidian is widely credited with rescuing hundreds using back roads he knew by heart.

The Wider Map

Key sites of October 7

The same morning, the whole Gaza envelope was attacked at once — by land and by sea. These are the places a visitor passes near on the way to Re'im, in a line each. Figures are approximate and widely reported.

Zikim beach

seaborne · ~17 killed

Naval commandos landed by boat on the coast north of Gaza. Most of the flotilla was destroyed at sea — one of the few assaults repelled at its entry point.

Sderot

~53 killed

Gunmen seized the town's police station and held it for a day; it was demolished to end the siege. The ruin is now a memorial and heroes' park.

Kibbutz Be'eri

~130 killed · ~30 taken

One of the worst-hit communities; recapturing it took the IDF days. Much of the kibbutz was destroyed.

Kibbutz Kfar Aza

~62 killed · ~19 taken

Overrun within an hour; a symbol of the border communities caught unprotected at dawn.

Kibbutz Nir Oz

~47 killed · ~76 taken

Proportionally the hardest hit — about a quarter of residents killed or abducted, one of the day's highest hostage tolls.

Nahal Oz

~68 killed · ~10 taken

The IDF base — home to the young surveillance spotters who had warned for months — and its adjacent community were overwhelmed.

Kibbutz Nir Am

0 residents killed

A rare successful defence: coordinator Inbal Rabin-Lieberman armed the standby squad and, with a Golani company, held the gate.

Netiv HaAsara

~15–20 killed

The moshav closest to Gaza's northern edge, struck in the first minutes of the assault.

On the ground today · The Ashkelon seafront

Where the sea attack came ashore

These photographs were taken by our own team at the Avenue of Remembrance (Sderat HaZikaron) on the Ashkelon seafront — the Mediterranean shore, beside the power-station stacks, along which the seaborne assault came in near Zikim at first light. Its dedication reads: “In memory of those murdered on the 7th of October.” This is what your group will stand before on the day.

A promenade of remembrance by the sea

Along the Ashkelon shore, a walkway of wave-crested steles now carries the portrait and life story of each person killed — set against the same water the attackers crossed at dawn. The twin stacks on the horizon are the Ashkelon power station, on the coast just north of the Zikim landing point.

The entrance monument. Its inscription names the site — “Avenue of Remembrance, in memory of those murdered on 7 October” — beside the emblem of the city of Ashkelon. A companion plaque recalls that at dawn that Saturday attackers came from the sea onto the southern beaches, and honours those who had come only to rest by the shore, closing simply: “May their memory be blessed.”
Bearing witness, in person. To stand on this shore is to grasp what a map cannot — how close the sea is, how ordinary the beach, and how the memory of that morning is now woven into the coastline itself.

Aftermath & Remembrance

How the world remembers

The Memorial at Re'im

Established by the victims' families with the Jewish National Fund (KKL) on the site itself — photographs of each person, Israeli flags, a planted memorial forest, and red anemone sculptures. It has become one of the most-visited sites in the country.

The "Car Wall" at Tkuma

West of Moshav Tkuma, roughly 1,600 burned and bullet-scarred vehicles — many from the Nova site and Route 232 — were gathered into a wall of remembrance. They were first collected so authorities could recover and identify victims' remains; the site became one of the principal memorials to the massacre.

The Nova Exhibition

"The Moment Music Stood Still" recreates the festival — tents, dance floor, recovered belongings. First shown at Expo Tel Aviv, it has toured New York, Los Angeles, Miami, Washington, Buenos Aires, Berlin and more, drawing over 600,000 visitors. Proceeds support the Tribe of Nova Foundation.

The films

We Will Dance Again (2024) won an Emmy for Outstanding Current Affairs Documentary; Supernova: The Music Festival Massacre and #NOVA assemble survivor testimony and attendees' own footage into an enduring record.

The Car Wall, Tkuma. A mountain of rusted, burned vehicles — cleared from the roads and the festival field, and now stacked as a single, staggering monument to the scale of that morning.
Every car, a story. Families have marked individual vehicles with portraits and flags — a face and a name returned to the metal that carried their loved ones.
Flags for the fallen. One car flies banners for those it remembers — among them Gaya Halifa, 24, and Ben Shimoni, 31, who escaped the festival but drove back again and again, saving nine people before he was killed.
"Am Yisrael Chai" — the People of Israel live. A mosaic and the "Remember" initiative invite visitors to scan a code, read each victim's story, and light a candle in their memory. Red anemones — the flower of this land — bloom in ceramic around the stones.

Visiting Today

Coming to bear witness

The Nova Festival Victims Memorial welcomes visitors who come to pay their respects. If you are travelling to the region now, here is what to know.

Cost

Free. The memorial is an open-air site maintained by the families and JNF/KKL.

Hours

Open-air and effectively accessible around the clock; daylight visits (~08:00–17:00) are recommended.

Getting there

By car via Route 232, at the Re'im parking lot. There is no direct public transport — drive or join a guided tour.

Coordinates

31.39778°N, 34.47167°E
Plus Code 9FXC+92, Re'im.

  • This is an active place of mourning — families are often present. Move gently and quietly, and photograph respectfully.
  • Dress modestly and bring a passport — the wider border area has checkpoints.
  • Nearby kibbutzim (Be'eri, Kfar Aza, Nir Oz) are generally restricted — access usually requires an authorised or guided visit.
  • This is a border region. Check current conditions and Home Front Command guidance before and during your visit.
Two things are often confused: the Memorial at Re'im is the free outdoor site described here; the Nova Exhibition is the paid, travelling museum installation shown in cities abroad. This guide is about visiting the site itself.
