Leg 7 · Jerusalem — Yitzhak Navon

Arrival, 80 metres beneath the city

The train stops at Yitzhak Navon — named for Israel's fifth president — one of the deepest railway stations in the world, carved ~80 m into the bedrock of Jerusalem. A watercolor skyline and the blue-and-white flag welcome you up into the City of Gold.

The Deepest StationUp out of the rock. Banks of soaring escalators run through a gleaming tunnel to raise you the full ~80 m to street level — the deepest heavy-rail station in the Middle East, and one of the deepest on earth.

Plot twist · true story

The great train mix-up 😵‍💫

Full confession: we missed the direct train. Again. (Not the first this week…) But the wrong train turned into the best kind of detour — a scenic loop through the hills via Modi'in, the ancient home of the Maccabees themselves.

Train No. 585 — the accidental scenic route

Instead of the express, we ended up on the train that winds via Modi'in Center → Pa'ate Modi'in → Jerusalem–Yitzhak Navon (18:06 · 18:13 · arriving 18:35). A longer way up — and a happy accident, because it carried us straight through one of the most storied towns in Jewish history.

The wrong-right trainTo JLM-Navon, the long way. The onboard screen tells the tale — 585, routed through Modi'in before the final climb to Jerusalem. Missed the express; caught history instead.
Modi'in–Center · מודיעין מרכזWhere the Maccabees came from. This modern city rises beside ancient Modi'in — birthplace of the Hasmonean revolt of 167 BCE, when Mattathias and his sons, the Maccabees, rose up and rededicated the Temple. The very story of Hanukkah began on these hills.
“In the days of Mattathias… and his sons, the Maccabees” — the miracle we light candles for each winter began right here, on the road we accidentally took.— the Hanukkah story, from Modi'in

Moral of the story: in Israel, even the trains you miss seem to know where they're taking you. 🕎

Custom & Meaning

Why you always go up to Jerusalem

For three thousand years, in every direction on the map, the language has been the same: you ascend to Jerusalem. Here are the small sacred details that travelled with us.

Aliyah la-Regel

עֲלִיָּה לָרֶגֶל

“Going up on foot” — the ancient pilgrimage. Three times a year Jews would ascend to the Temple in Jerusalem. Because the city sits high in the hills, and higher still in the soul, one never travels down or across to Jerusalem — only up.

Rav-Kav

רַב-קַו

The single smartcard that carries a traveller across all of Israel's public transport — light rail, city buses and the intercity trains. One green-and-purple tap, from the streets of Tel Aviv to the platform bound for Jerusalem.

Tefillin

תְּפִילִּין

Two small black leather boxes, bound to the arm and head, holding hand-written parchment scrolls of the Shema — “Hear O Israel…” (Deuteronomy 6). To lay them on the train is to carry the words up to Jerusalem, bound close to heart and mind.

The City Below Your Feet

Jerusalem — three thousand years

No city on earth has been more loved, lost and rebuilt. Sacred to Judaism, Christianity and Islam, Jerusalem has been destroyed and raised again more times than almost any place in history. A brief walk down through the layers:

c. 1800 BCE
A city on the ridge
A Canaanite / Jebusite town clings to a narrow spur above the Gihon Spring — the core we now call the City of David. Its name, likely “Ir-Shalem,” carries the root of shalom, peace.
c. 1000 BCE
King David's capital
David captures the city and makes it the capital of a united Israel. His son Solomon builds the First Temple on Mount Moriah — the heart of Jewish worship.
586 BCE
The First Destruction
Babylon razes the Temple and exiles the people. By the rivers of Babylon they weep — “If I forget thee, O Jerusalem…” (Psalm 137).
516 BCE
The Second Temple
Returning exiles rebuild. Five centuries later Herod the Great vastly expands the Temple Mount on a colossal platform of stone — whose western retaining wall still stands today.
70 CE
Rome and the ruin
Rome destroys the Second Temple. Only the great retaining walls survive — the western one becoming the holiest place of Jewish prayer: the Kotel.
638 CE
The city of three faiths
Under Byzantine, then Islamic rule the city becomes sacred to Christianity and Islam too; the Dome of the Rock rises on the Mount in 691.
1099 – 1537
Crusaders, then Ottomans
Crusader kingdoms come and go. In 1537–41 Suleiman the Magnificent builds the golden limestone walls and gates that still ring the Old City — including Jaffa Gate.
1948 – 1967
Divided, then reunited
The modern State of Israel is born in 1948; for 19 years the Old City lies beyond a border. In June 1967 Jerusalem is reunited and Jews return to pray at the Wall for the first time in a generation.
Today
The City of Gold
Built almost entirely of pale “Jerusalem stone” that glows honey-gold at sunset, the city rises ~754 m in the Judean Hills — the living destination at the top of this journey.

The Destination

The Kotel — the Western Wall

The last and holiest place at the end of the climb. Not a wall of the Temple itself, but the wall that has held the mountain up for two thousand years.

הַכּוֹתֶל הַמַּעֲרָבִי · HaKotel HaMa'aravi

The Western Wall is a surviving section of the massive retaining wall Herod the Great built (~19 BCE) to enlarge the Temple Mount — not part of the Temple building, but the mighty stonework that supported its plaza. When Rome destroyed the Temple in 70 CE, this wall endured. For centuries Jews have come here to pray at the spot closest to where the Holy of Holies once stood; tradition holds the divine presence, the Shechinah, has never departed from it.

The stones

The visible wall rises about 19 m above the plaza, part of a length of some 488 m. Its great lower courses are original Herodian stones, each cut with a fine recessed border.

The notes

Visitors fold tiny prayers and press them into the cracks between the stones — kvitlach. More than a million a year are placed; twice a year they are gathered with care and buried on the Mount of Olives.

The plaza

An open square before the Wall, with separate sections for men and women to pray. Day and night, in every weather, it is never empty.

“I rejoiced when they said unto me: Let us go up to the house of the Lord. Our feet are standing within thy gates, O Jerusalem.”— Psalm 122
“If I forget thee, O Jerusalem, let my right hand forget her cunning.”— Psalm 137

Around the Wall

The Old City — gates & holy ground

The Kotel sits inside a square kilometre ringed by golden walls, threaded with the deepest history in the Jewish world. From Jaffa Gate you walk in and down toward the Wall.

The Gateway

Jaffa Gate

Built by Suleiman in 1538, the great western gate faces the old road to Jaffa and the coast — the very direction we arrived from. In 1898 the wall beside it was breached to welcome Kaiser Wilhelm II; today it's the traveller's entrance into the Old City.

The Holiest Place

Temple Mount · Har HaBayit

The raised platform above the Kotel — site of both Temples and, by tradition, the Foundation Stone of the world. The holiest site in Judaism; the golden Dome of the Rock crowns it now.

The Four Quarters

Inside the walls

Eight gates pierce Suleiman's walls; within, the city divides into the Jewish, Muslim, Christian and Armenian Quarters. The path to the Wall runs down through the Jewish Quarter's honey-stone lanes.

The Origin

The City of David

Just outside the southern wall lies the original city — ~3,000 years old — with Hezekiah's Tunnel still carrying spring water through the rock, hewn in the 8th century BCE.

The Vista

The Mount of Olives

Across the Kidron Valley, the ridge holds the oldest continuously used Jewish cemetery on earth — and the classic golden view back across the Old City and the Mount.

The Last Steps

Reaching the Kotel

From Yitzhak Navon station: hop the Jerusalem Light Rail Red Line toward City Hall / Damascus Gate, or walk up to Jaffa Gate — then down through the Jewish Quarter to the Western Wall plaza.

From the Platform to the Wall

How to reach the Kotel

Stepping off at Yitzhak Navon, ~80 m underground — here's the last stretch up to the Western Wall.

1 · Rise up

Ride the long escalators and lift up out of the deepest station in the Middle East into central Jerusalem.

2 · Red Line

Board the Jerusalem Light Rail toward City Hall / Damascus Gate — a short, scenic hop across the modern city.

3 · Jaffa Gate

Enter the Old City through Jaffa Gate and walk down through the Jewish Quarter's stone lanes.

4 · The Wall

Arrive at the Western Wall plaza. Open 24 hours, every day, free. Modest dress; heads covered for men.

  • Write a note beforehand — you can press it into the cracks of the Wall.
  • Sunset and night are the most beautiful hours — the stone turns to gold, then silver.
  • Separate men's and women's sections; a respectful hush across the whole plaza.
